Basic Questions to Ask and Spot Issues

Following are the basic questions you should ask while reviewing a resume.

  • Are there spelling or grammatical errors?
  • Are there gaps in employment?
  • Does the candidate “job hop”?
  • Is the resume formatted well?
  • Are there red flags?
Show More

Are there spelling or grammatical errors?

Ideally, a resume should have a formal language and professional tone.

Read through the resume to check for spelling and grammatical errors. You may use a computer spell checker or dictionary for checking online resumes. For printed resumes, you may get them proof read.

Are there gaps in employment?

An ideal candidate would seldom have gaps in employment and if so, they would have valid reasons for it.

In the resume, check the employment tenure for each company and calculate the gaps by looking at the joining and resigning dates.

Does the candidate “job hop”?

Check if the resume reflects short tenures with several employers. Candidates showing this trait are considered less loyal and unreliable.

In the resume, check the number of jobs the candidate has changed as against the employment tenure.

Is the resume formatted well?

Resumes with comfortable viewing formats are always appreciated. It sets the first hand impression of the candidate.

In the resume, check how the textual information flows. Check the font color and size, indentation and spacing of lines/paragraphs.

Are there red flags?

Identify critical areas of suspicion. It could be incorrect dates, non-specific educational qualifications, fabricated company information, ambiguous roles and responsibilities etc.

In the resume, note anything that seems unclear, vague, or uncommon. Note misspellings, incorrect dates, numbers or percentages, gaps in information and inadequate information.

Types of Visible Issues in a Resume

A resume can have glaring issues or visible issues that can be spotted easily at a place.

Following issues are considered as visible issues.

  • Grammatical mistakes
  • Incorrect spellings
  • Irrelevant information under a relevant heading (for example- listing hobbies under skills)
  • Informal language/wordings:
    • Setting expectations for bonus
    • Use of offensive / explicit language
    • Fake/offensive/funny email ID
  • Short work history under experience
  • Self-employment or running own set-up (validation should be done - when and how?)
  • Details too sketchy/vague or missing for roles and responsibilities held
  • Formatting consistency issues
  • Details missing /vagueness/very short explanation (where required)
  • Unexplained gaps in employment
  • Dates missing
